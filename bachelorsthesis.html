This study focuses on the Green Synthesis of Silver Nanoparticles (AgNPs) using Acacia catechu extract as a reducing agent. We characterized the synthesized nanoparticles and performed Molecular Docking to evaluate their potential as drug delivery carriers for antimalarial drugs like Atovaquone and Mefloquine.

1. Methodology: Green Synthesis

Silver nanoparticles were synthesized by reducing Silver Nitrate (AgNO3) with Acacia catechu (Kattha) powder extract. The reaction mixture changed from light apricot to dark brown, indicating the formation of nanoparticles.

  • Precursor: 1mM Silver Nitrate (AgNO3)
  • Reducing Agent: Acacia catechu extract (0.1g in 400ml distilled water)
  • Condition: Incubated in dark at Room Temperature

2. Characterization Results

A. UV-Visible Spectroscopy

The formation of AgNPs was confirmed by a strong Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) peak.

Figure 1: UV-Visible spectrum showing a characteristic peak at 463 nm [Source: Thesis Data].

B. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)

SEM analysis revealed the topology and size of the nanoparticles.

Figure 2: SEM images showing spherical nanoparticles with a size range of 17-25 nm.

C. X-Ray Diffraction (XRD)

XRD confirmed the crystalline nature of the nanoparticles (FCC structure) with an average crystallite size of ~18 nm.

Figure 3: XRD pattern showing peaks at (111), (200), (220), and (311) planes.

3. Computational Docking Studies

Using FlexX software, we docked 6 antimalarial drugs with the phytochemicals capping the nanoparticles. The goal was to find the best candidate for drug conjugation.

Figure 4: 3D Visualization of Atovaquone interacting with nanoparticle surface molecules via Hydrogen Bonding and Pi-Pi Stacking.

Key Findings:

  • Atovaquone: Showed the highest binding affinity, interacting with 7 out of 8 Kattha components.
  • Mefloquine: Demonstrated strong interaction, primarily through Pi-Cation bonds.

4. Conclusion

The study successfully demonstrated that Acacia catechu is an effective green reducing agent. The computational data suggests that these AgNPs are highly compatible with Atovaquone, making them a promising candidate for a novel antimalarial drug delivery system.
