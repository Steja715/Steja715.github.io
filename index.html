Project Overview

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a growing global health threat, particularly in Low- and Middle-Income Countries (LMICs). This study addresses the critical gap in AMR awareness by examining it through the lens of Health Literacy in Baddi, Himachal Pradesh—a major pharmaceutical industrial hub.

[cite_start]The research explores both the Demand Side (community knowledge and behavior) and the Supply Side (pharmacist dispensing practices) to understand the drivers of antibiotic misuse[cite: 14].

Part 1: Community Health Literacy (Demand Side)

We surveyed 450 residents using the Health Literacy Questionnaire (HLQ) and a custom AMR knowledge index. [cite_start]The study found that education was the strongest predictor of AMR knowledge (R² = 0.192), explaining 19% of the variance alone[cite: 388, 471].

Part 2: Pharmacist Practices (Supply Side)

Using a simulated client method, we approached 239 pharmacies presenting with symptoms of fever, diarrhea, or respiratory infection. [cite_start]The analysis revealed a heavy reliance on broad-spectrum antibiotics, often disregarding WHO stewardship guidelines[cite: 298, 606].

Heatmap of Dispensing Patterns

Pharmacists frequently recommended antibiotics for conditions where they were not necessary. [cite_start]For diarrheal cases, 56% of recommendations were for Ofloxacin (a broad-spectrum antibiotic) rather than ORS or Zinc[cite: 1074].

WHO AWaRe Classification Analysis

The study compared dispensed drugs against the WHO "Access, Watch, Reserve" (AWaRe) classification. [cite_start]65.2% of dispensed antibiotics belonged to the "Watch" category (higher resistance potential), far exceeding the WHO recommendation to limit these drugs[cite: 1153, 1154].

Conclusion & Impact

The study highlights a "reinforcing cycle" of misuse: low community health literacy drives demand for quick fixes, and unregulated pharmacy practices supply strong antibiotics to meet that demand. [cite_start]Interventions must target educational appraisal skills rather than just generic awareness, and enforce stricter regulations on pharmacy dispensing[cite: 1309, 1316].
