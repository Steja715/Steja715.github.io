AMR & Health Literacy

A Mixed-Methods Study on the Demand & Supply of Antibiotics in Baddi, India

R Studio SPSS Ophelia Framework Regression Modeling
450 Community Surveyed
239 Pharmacists Audited
65% "Watch" Antibiotics
57% Misuse Rate

Education Drives Awareness

One of the most critical findings of the study was the clear linear trend between Education Level and AMR Awareness scores.

[cite_start]As illustrated in the box plots, participants with graduate and postgraduate degrees scored significantly higher medians compared to those with no formal education[cite: 2337].

This trend confirms that lack of formal schooling is a primary barrier to understanding antibiotic risks. [cite_start]While Occupation (shown on the right) also showed variation, regression analysis revealed that Education was the true underlying driver[cite: 2340].

Figure 1: Box plots demonstrating the significant upward trend in AMR knowledge scores as Education level increases (Left), compared with Occupation groups (Right).

The "Critical Appraisal" Gap

Using the Ophelia HLQ Framework, we dissected which specific skills predict better antibiotic usage.

The analysis revealed that general "Social Support" or "Engagement" had little impact. Instead, the strongest predictor was Critical Appraisal (Domain 5)—the ability to question and verify health information.

This insight shifts the focus of future interventions: we don't just need to "spread awareness"; we need to teach people how to evaluate the information they receive.

Figure 2: Correlation magnitude of 9 Health Literacy domains. 'Appraisal of Health Information' (Red bar) is the strongest predictor of AMR awareness.

The Supply Side: Pharmacist Behavior

While the community demands antibiotics due to misconceptions, pharmacists fuel this fire by supplying them without restriction. Our audit of 239 pharmacies revealed a dangerous reliance on high-strength drugs.

Figure 3: 65.2% of all dispensed antibiotics were "Watch" category.

The "Watch" Category Crisis

The WHO classifies antibiotics into Access, Watch, and Reserve. "Watch" antibiotics have a higher potential for resistance and should be limited.

In Baddi, 65.2% of all antibiotics dispensed belonged to this high-risk Watch category (Red), far exceeding the safer "Access" group (Green).

[cite_start]This imbalance is a direct threat to public health, accelerating the development of superbugs in the region[cite: 2350, 2351].

Misuse Across All Ages & Illnesses

The pattern of misuse was not random—it was systematic.

Diarrhoea (Left Chart): This was the most mismanaged condition. [cite_start]Pharmacists recommended Watch category antibiotics in 76.1% of cases, disregarding guidelines for ORS and Zinc[cite: 2353].

[cite_start]Pediatric Risk (Right Chart): Even for infants (0-5 years), nearly 60% of recommendations were for high-strength Watch antibiotics, exposing young children to unnecessary risks[cite: 2358].

Figure 4: Stacked bar charts showing the dominance of Watch antibiotics (Orange) across all Illness types and Age groups.

Conclusion

This study highlights a reinforcing cycle: Low Education limits the community's ability to appraise risks, driving demand for quick fixes. Pharmacists meet this demand with powerful, inappropriate antibiotics to retain customers.

Effective policy must target both sides: Educational interventions to improve "Critical Appraisal" skills, and stricter regulatory enforcement on pharmacy dispensing.
