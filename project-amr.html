Project Background

Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) is currently killing more people than malaria or HIV. [cite_start]Misuse of "miracle drugs" is accelerating this burden[cite: 4661]. [cite_start]This study focuses on Baddi, Himachal Pradesh, one of Asia's biggest pharmaceutical hubs with over 2,000 factories[cite: 4670].

[cite_start]Despite being a pharma hub, the region faces challenges like poor hygiene, open drainage, and high antibiotic residue in the Sirsa river[cite: 4672, 4674]. This project investigates the link between Health Literacy (demand side) and Pharmacist Dispensing (supply side).

Part 1: Community Survey Results

[cite_start]We utilized the Ophelia HLQ (Health Literacy Questionnaire) to assess 9 distinct domains of health literacy[cite: 4686]. The sample was diverse, covering various education levels and occupations.

Demographics Breakdown

[cite_start]The study found a clear trend: Education was the strongest predictor of AMR awareness (19.2% variance), while Occupation contributed 9.5% variance[cite: 4708, 4709].

Source of Medicine

A critical finding was the source of antibiotics. [cite_start]56.9% of respondents rely on pharmacists or self-medication rather than a doctor's prescription, indicating a high level of misuse[cite: 4701].

Part 2: Pharmacist Prescribing Behaviors

Using a simulated patient approach, we approached 239 pharmacists with symptoms of fever, diarrhea, or respiratory infection. [cite_start]The results revealed a dangerous dominance of "Watch" category drugs (broad-spectrum antibiotics carrying higher resistance risk)[cite: 4713].

Key Findings:

    [cite_start]
  • Fluoroquinolones Dominance: Ofloxacin and Norfloxacin comprised 36.9% of all antibiotic recommendations[cite: 4714].

  • Diarrheal Cases: 76.1% of prescriptions for diarrhea were broad-spectrum "Watch" antibiotics, ignoring standard guidelines[cite: 4717].

  • Pediatric Risk: Even for infants (0-5 years), Watch category drugs accounted for 59.1% of recommendations[cite: 4721].

Conclusion & Impact

The study highlights that AMR is not just a biological issue but a social one. Low health literacy in the community drives demand for quick fixes, and pharmacists fulfill this demand with strong, inappropriate antibiotics. Effective interventions must target educational appraisal skills and enforce stricter dispensing regulations in pharmaceutical hubs like Baddi.
