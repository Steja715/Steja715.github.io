Project Overview

Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) is a global crisis, often accelerated by misuse in Low- and Middle-Income Countries. [cite_start]This study focused on Baddi, Himachal Pradesh, one of Asia's largest pharmaceutical hubs[cite: 2307]. [cite_start]Despite being a production center, the region faces significant public health challenges including open drainage and high antibiotic residue in local rivers[cite: 2309, 2311].

[cite_start]The study investigates the disconnect between Demand (Community Knowledge) and Supply (Pharmacist Dispensing)[cite: 14].

Research Objectives

[cite_start]

The study was guided by four primary research questions to bridge the gap between social determinants and clinical outcomes [cite: 105-108]:

RQ 1: Health Literacy Levels

What are the levels of health literacy among community members in Baddi, and how do these influence awareness regarding antibiotic use?

RQ 2: Socio-Demographic Drivers

Which factors (age, gender, education, occupation) are most strongly associated with AMR awareness?

RQ 3: Pharmacist Behavior

How do pharmacists dispense antibiotics for common illnesses, and how does this align with WHO AWaRe classifications?

RQ 4: The Interaction

How do community demand and supply-side practices together shape the dynamics of antibiotic misuse?

Methodology: The Ophelia Framework

[cite_start]We utilized the Ophelia (Optimising Health Literacy and Access) Health Literacy Questionnaire (HLQ)[cite: 171]. Unlike simple knowledge tests, this tool assesses 9 distinct dimensions of how people process health information:

1 Feeling understood & supported by healthcare providers
2 Having sufficient info to manage health
3 Actively managing own health
4 Social support for health
5 Appraisal of health information (Critical Thinking)
6 Ability to actively engage with providers
7 Navigating the healthcare system
8 Ability to find good health information
9 Understanding health info well enough to act

Part 2: Pharmacist Prescribing

Using a simulated patient approach (Mystery Shopper), we audited 239 pharmacies. [cite_start]The results showed a dangerous over-reliance on "Watch" category antibiotics (broad-spectrum drugs with higher resistance potential)[cite: 1153].

  • 72.8% of antibiotics were dispensed without a valid prescription[cite: 1195].

  • Diarrheal Cases: 76.1% of prescriptions were for broad-spectrum antibiotics like Ofloxacin, ignoring guidelines for ORS/Zinc[cite: 1179].

Conclusion & Impact

This study demonstrates that AMR is not just a biological challenge but a social one. Low health literacy—specifically the inability to appraise information (Domain 5)—drives demand for quick fixes. Pharmacists meet this demand with powerful, inappropriate antibiotics to retain customers.

[cite_start]Key Recommendation: Interventions must move beyond general awareness to target specific appraisal skills and enforce strict dispensing regulations in industrial hubs[cite: 1316, 1317].
